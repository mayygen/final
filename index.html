Asian Fetishization:

Racial fetishization comes in many shapes and forms. It can be thought of as the act of making someone an object of sexual desire based on some aspect of their identity. Asian fetishization, particularly of Asian women, is often associated with ideologies of submissiveness, obedience, and hypersexualization. Moreover, Asian fetishization ties closely into appropriation of the culture.

History

The fetishization and sexualization of Asian Women is not a new phenomenon. Vox’s Li Zhou reports, “Asian American women have been exoticized and fetishized as sexual partners as far back as the 1800s.” Asian patriarchal culture created a twisted beauty standard for Asian women. They are seen as submissive, young, skinny, pale, and look easy to be controlled. This beauty standard is completely formed by the male gaze.

In America, Even before the Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882, which banned Chinese immigrants from becoming US citizens, the US had passed the Page Act of 1875, which ultimately banned the importation of Asian women, who were feared to be engaging in prostitution in the country, whether they were or not.

Another potential origin of Eastern Fetishization comes from the emergence of films and artwork after US-led wars in Asian countries is when the trope of the hypersexual but docile Asian woman really took hold in America. For example, with anime (which has been steadily gathering a worldwide fanbase, since the release of Akira in 1988) there exist recurring tropes that have adverse effects on women. There often exist female characters that are hyper-sexualized for the purpose of fan service. Moreover, the women in some of these series often serve no purpose other than to look sexy and keep men interested.

Associated Terminology

Stereotype: a widely held but fixed and oversimplified image or idea of a particular type of person or thing.

Asian-fishing: when a not Asian individual purposely makes themselves look like East-Asian for the aesthetic.

Yellow-fever: The derogatory term yellow fever is sometimes used to describe the fetishisation of East Asians by people of other ethnicities, especially among non-Asians, as well as having a preference for dating people of East Asian origin.

Cultural appropriation: the unacknowledged or inappropriate adoption of the customs, practices, ideas, etc. of one people or society by members of another and typically more dominant people or society.

Examples of Asian Fetishization

Tattoos: Ariana Grande attempted to ink an ode to her hit single, 7 Rings, but it backfired after as the Japanese characters actually translated to shichirin: a small charcoal grill. People getting tattoos of languages/cultures they often have no connection to (commonly Chinese, Japanese, and Korean characters) is not a new thing. This is a form of cultural appropriation as people are adopting aspects of another culture for the sake of aesthetic purposes.

Makeup: makeup-artists say the formerly trending “fox-eye makeup look” is innocent enough, but many Asians say it's racially insensitive and hints at cultural appropriation.

Fashion: Cultural appropriation through fashion is common and often sidelined. Much of traditional clothing is not simply functional or ornamental but is infused with meaning, and is part of the identity of the Indigenous communities that use it. Copying/consuming designs without consideration for their underlying cultural significance can erode the identity of a whole community.

Keziah Daum, an 18-year-old senior at Woods Cross High School, Utah, received backlash after she posted photos of her dressed in a red qipao, a traditional Chinese dress, on prom night. “For it to simply be subject to American consumerism and cater to a white audience, is parallel to colonial ideology,” an Asian-American student on twitter wrote. At the time, she said, she was only focused on the modesty and subtle elegance of the dress. She said she was not aware of the cultural context that’s deeply embedded with the garment’s history, one that can be traced back to the 17th century. The qipao, or cheongsam (in Cantonese), is a symbol of liberation and sensuality, it shapes and has been shaped by the shifting silhouettes of Chinese society through the tides of time.

How can we appreciate and not appropriate?

The first step is to fully understand why this is an issue at all. Cultural appropriation’s toxicity is hard to fully comprehend if you don’t have an understanding of white supremacy. Adopting parts of oppressed cultures while actual members of said cultures are demonized is definitely tough ground. Admittedly, there is a fine line between appropriation and appreciation, and many grow frustrated trying to differentiate. Is there a right way to consume a culture without appropriating it? Everything is situational, but there exist common factors that can guide one’s consumption. For example, learning the background of the aspect of the culture you want to consume. Some things you can ask yourself if you are not sure if you are being respectful is:

1. Am I reducing the value of whatever it is I'm consuming? (i.e. reducing cultural clothing to a "fashion statement")

2. Am I in an environment where this is appropriate? (possibly regarding history of the entity)

3. Are people of this culture the ones who are profiting off of this?
